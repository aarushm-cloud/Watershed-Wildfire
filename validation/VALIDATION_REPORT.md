## 1. The question (pre-registered)

> Does a **simple** terrain-+-burn flow-routing screen, applied to a burned area, correctly **rank** the watersheds that actually produced damaging debris flows **above** those that didn't — for a fire with documented outcomes?

This is a go/no-go gate, decided *before* building anything elaborate:

- **PASS →** the simplified approach is good enough for a *screening* tool. Commit.
- **FAIL →** the simple model can't even rank known outcomes. Do not ship a naive model over people's homes; upgrade the routing method or fall back to Plan B.

### Why Montecito
The 2017 Thomas Fire burned the Santa Ynez front above Montecito, CA. Three weeks later, on 9 Jan 2018, an intense rain cell triggered debris flows from the burned catchments that killed 23 people and damaged 400+ homes. It is the most thoroughly documented post-fire debris-flow disaster in the U.S., so the ground truth (which watersheds flowed) is public and authoritative — the single best validation case available.

### Pre-registered pass bar (fixed before looking at any result)
1. **≥ 70%** of the documented debris-flow watersheds fall in the screen's **top tercile** (top third) of risk, **AND**
2. the screen's **#1-ranked** watershed is one that **actually flowed**.

### Pre-registered scoring formula (never changed during analysis)
```
score(basin) = mean_burn_severity(basin) × mean_slope(basin) × contributing_area_km²
```
Deliberately simple — a screening heuristic, not a physics simulation. The entire point of the gate is to learn whether *this* is good enough to rank real outcomes.

## 2. Data — all real, all independently sourced

| Input | Source | Details |
|---|---|---|
| **DEM** | USGS 3DEP 10 m via `elevation.nationalmap.gov` ImageServer `exportImage` | Clipped to the Montecito front, output **EPSG:32611 (UTM 11N)**, 1413 × 1295 px @ 10.0 m. Elevation range −1.7 → 1178.6 m (Pacific shoreline → Santa Ynez crest). |
| **Burn severity** | Thomas Fire **BAER Soil Burn Severity** via the USFS/GeoPlatform image service | Mosaic **raster-locked to the Thomas Fire raster only** (objectid 3248) so no later fire contaminates the area. Classes 1–4; 25.2% of the grid burned, dominated by moderate (17.5%). |
| **Assets (values-at-risk)** | OpenStreetMap buildings (Overpass) | **12,066** building points across the Montecito alluvial fans. |
| **Ground truth (flowed watersheds)** | Kean et al. 2019 (*Geosphere* v15 n4, p1140; USGS data release DOI 10.5066/P9JQJU0E) + named-creek channels from OSM + debris-basin excavation volumes | See §6 — this is the one input we had to substitute. |

**Burn-weight mapping** (BAER soil-burn-severity class → 0–1 weight): `1→0.0, 2→0.33, 3→0.67, 4→1.0` (unburned/outside-perimeter → 0).

### Truth caveat (important)
The canonical truth — USGS *inundation polygons* (Kean et al. 2019) — lives only inside a single **2.04 GB** ScienceBase zip. Two download attempts stalled near completion (the server does not support HTTP range/resume; a stalled connection produced a truncated, invalid zip), after which ScienceBase **rate-limited our IP entirely** (every request, even the 9 KB metadata file, returned empty replies). Rather than keep retrying a flaky 2 GB transfer, we substituted an equivalent, independently-published ground truth:

- **Kean et al. 2019's five documented flowed watersheds:** Montecito, Oak, San Ysidro, Buena Vista, and Romero Creeks.
- Their **channel geometries** pulled from OSM (authoritative named features).
- Cross-checked against **post-event debris-basin excavation volumes** (hard physical evidence a creek flowed): Romero 33,000 m³, Cold Spring 19,000 m³, San Ysidro 17,000 m³, Montecito 1,800 m³.

This is coarser than the polygons (watershed-level, not metre-level inundation), but it is the *observed* outcome from the peer-reviewed record, not anything derived from our own model — so it is a legitimate, if blunter, validation target.

## 3. Method (the screen)

1. **Hydrology** (pysheds): fill pits → fill depressions → resolve flats → D8 flow direction → flow accumulation.
2. **Canyon-mouth outlets:** find every channel cell (accumulation > 500 cells ≈ 0.05 km²) that crosses the **150 m mountain-front contour** going downhill. The 150 m contour is the topographic break where these creeks leave the mountains onto the developed fans (chosen on terrain grounds, *before* any truth was loaded; building elevations cluster below ~170 m).
3. **Delineate** each outlet's upslope catchment; discard catchments < 0.1 km²; keep only those whose channel reaches within 600 m of the building layer (i.e., actually drain toward Montecito). Larger catchments claim their cells first to prevent double-counting tributaries → **32 candidate basins**.
4. **Score** each basin with the pre-registered `burn × slope × area` formula and rank.
5. **Truth overlay:** assign each documented flowed creek to the basin whose canyon-mouth outlet lies on that creek's channel (nearest outlet-to-channel, ≤ 250 m). Purely geometric — no hand-placed points.

### 4.3 How strong is the separation?

This is more than a bare pass — the screen separates flowed from non-flowed almost completely:

- **Rank-AUC = 0.987.** Across all 6 × 26 = 156 flowed/non-flowed basin pairs, the flowed basin scores higher in 154 of them. (A coin-flip screen would give 0.5; a perfect one 1.0.)
- **Mean score:** flowed basins **1.619** vs non-flowed **0.066** — a **~25×** gap.
- **Mean burn fraction:** flowed **0.55** vs non-flowed **0.12**. The flowed catchments are exactly the heavily-burned, steep, large ones — which is the physical mechanism the screen encodes.
- The **top 5 ranks are all flowed**; the 24 unburned/low basins collapse to score ≈ 0.

### 4.4 The one real miss

The separation is not perfect. **Oak Creek** (flowed, but a small 0.55 km² catchment) scores **0.131**, below **two Toro Canyon basins** (non-flowed, 0.63–0.67). So:

- `min flowed score (0.131) < max non-flowed score (0.672)` — the classes overlap at the margin.
- Cause: the `× area` term lets two larger, moderately-burned eastern catchments (Toro Canyon) outscore a small flowed one. Toro Canyon is east of the main impact zone and did not produce documented damaging flows in 2018.

This is the screen's honest weakness: it can rank a large unburned-ish catchment above a small flowed one. It still clears the pre-registered bar comfortably because the bar is tercile-based, not strict pairwise separation.

## 6. The load-bearing judgment call (full transparency)

The only subjective decision in the whole gate, and it determines the top-1 criterion:

**OSM splits the Montecito Creek watershed into three named canyons** — its two upper forks, *Cold Spring Creek* and *Hot Springs Creek*, and the lower main stem *Montecito Creek*. Our basin outlets sit at the 150 m apex, i.e. **on the forks**, so:

- Basin #1 matches the *Cold Spring* channel (11 m) and basin #4 matches *Hot Springs* (5 m).
- The OSM *"Montecito Creek"* line is the post-confluence main stem, **down on the fan below the apex**, so it matched no above-apex basin (403 m → "UNMATCHED" in the log). This is a naming artifact, not a missing watershed.

We classified Cold Spring and Hot Springs as **flowed** (part of the documented Montecito Creek watershed). Justification is independent of our model: **19,000 m³ of debris was excavated from the Cold Spring debris basin** after the event — hard physical proof that catchment produced a major debris flow.

### Sensitivity to this call
- **As classified (Cold Spring/Hot Springs = forks of flowed Montecito Ck):** top-1 = flowed → **PASS**.
- **Strict literal reading (only the 5 OSM names "as-is", Cold Spring/Hot Springs treated as unrelated):** flowed basins would be ranks {2, 3, 5, 8}; capture in top tercile still 4/4 = 100% (PASS), but **#1 (Cold Spring) would be labeled "did not flow" → top-1 FAILS → overall FAIL.**

So the verdict flips on this one classification. We are confident the "flowed" classification is the factually correct one (the debris-basin volume settles it), but the result should be read with this explicitly on the table.

## 7. Limitations

1. **Truth granularity.** Watershed-level (did creek X flow?), not the metre-level USGS inundation polygons we couldn't download. The screen's *ranking* is validated; its downstream *inundation footprint* is not.
2. **Marginal mis-ranking.** Toro Canyon (non-flowed) outranks Oak Creek (flowed) — the `× area` term can dominate burn signal for larger catchments.
3. **Single event, single fire.** One disaster validates the approach; it does not establish transferability to other ranges, rain forcing, or fire regimes. A second case (e.g., a Cascades or Front Range post-fire event) would strengthen the claim.
4. **Burn input is class-based** (BAER 4-class SBS), not continuous dNBR; the burn term is therefore coarse-grained.
5. **DEM hydrology** uses depression-filling, which can create minor artifacts near the domain's north (crest) edge; not material to the basins of interest.

---

## 8. Bottom line

A deliberately simple `burn × slope × area` screen, run blind on real DEM + burn-severity data, ranks the catchments that actually produced the deadly 2018 Montecito debris flows into the **top 5 of 32** (rank-AUC 0.987), clearing both pre-registered criteria. **The screening approach is validated — commit to it.** Track the two known weaknesses (small flowed catchments; large non-flowed catchments) and, for production, upgrade the truth target to the USGS inundation polygons and add a second independent validation event.

### Engineering notes (bugs fixed during the run)
- **NumPy 2.x:** pysheds calls the removed `np.in1d`; shimmed to `np.isin`.
- **pysheds `catchment()` (decisive):** coordinate-mode (`xytype="coordinate"`) silently returned **0 km²** for valid outlets, which had been deleting the two largest flowed basins (San Ysidro, Montecito) before they could be ranked. Switching to index mode (`xytype="index"`, col/row) fixed it. Verified against the known 39.4 km² master outlet (0.00 → 39.19 km²).
